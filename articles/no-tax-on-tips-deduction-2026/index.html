No Tax on Tips: How the New $25,000 Deduction Actually Works

"No tax on tips" became a real, functioning deduction for the first time with the One Big Beautiful Bill Act — but the actual mechanics are narrower than the campaign-trail phrase suggests, and several details catch people off guard.

It's a Deduction, Not an Exemption

Despite the "no tax" branding, OBBBA Section 224 created a deduction for qualified tip income, not a blanket exemption. You still receive and report your tips as income; the deduction then reduces your taxable income by up to the qualifying amount. It's an above-the-line deduction, meaning you get it whether you take the standard deduction or itemize — it doesn't require giving up your standard deduction to claim it.

The $25,000 Cap and the Phase-Out

You can deduct up to $25,000 of qualified tip income per return for tax years 2025 through 2028. The deduction phases out once your Modified Adjusted Gross Income exceeds $150,000 (single) or $300,000 (Married Filing Jointly), shrinking by $100 for every $1,000 of MAGI above the threshold. Married Filing Separately returns don't qualify for this deduction at all.

Who Actually Qualifies

The final regulations confirm an exhaustive list of more than 70 qualifying occupations — servers, bartenders, hairdressers, golf caddies, taxi and rideshare drivers, and similar roles where tipping has been customary and regular as of December 31, 2024. A qualifying tip must be paid voluntarily by the customer in cash (or cash-equivalent); mandatory service charges, automatic gratuities, and tips paid in digital assets don't count as qualified tips for this deduction.

New Reporting Requirement Starting 2026

Beginning with the 2026 tax year, employers must separately report qualified tip income on Form W-2 (and payers must do the same on relevant 1099 forms), so the IRS can verify the deduction against a specific, isolated figure rather than your total wages. Starting in 2026, only this separately-reported qualified amount is what you can actually deduct.

💡 Keep your own tip log — date, shift, and amount — as a backup. If the separately-reported figure on your W-2 or 1099 looks lower than what you actually received in tips, you'll want your own records to sort out the discrepancy with whoever handles your payroll before you file.

FICA and State Tax Still Apply

This deduction only affects your federal income tax. Social Security and Medicare tax (FICA) still apply to all your tip income in full, with no exception — your employer still withholds and remits FICA on tips the same as always. Most states with their own income tax also don't automatically conform to this new federal deduction, meaning your state return may still tax tip income even after the federal deduction shields it.

Self-Employed Tipped Workers

The deduction isn't limited to W-2 employees — self-employed people in qualifying tipped occupations (an independent hairdresser or a rideshare driver operating as a sole proprietor, for example) can also claim it on qualifying tip income reported through their business, subject to the same cap and phase-out. This is separate from the self-employment tax owed on that same income, which this deduction does not reduce.

A Worked Example

A server with $18,000 in qualified tip income and $170,000 in total MAGI (single) is $20,000 over the $150,000 threshold, reducing the deduction by $100 × 20 = $2,000. Instead of the full $18,000, they can deduct $16,000 of it (since $18,000 minus $2,000 stays under the $25,000 cap anyway, the phase-out is what actually limits them here, not the cap).

Common Questions

Does this apply to self-employed tipped workers too? Generally yes, if the income qualifies as tip income from a customarily-tipped occupation and is properly reported — self-employment tax still applies regardless of this income tax deduction.

Will this deduction still exist after 2028? Not automatically — it's currently written into law only through the 2028 tax year; whether it's extended depends on future legislation.

Do I need a Social Security Number to claim it? Yes — a valid SSN is required on the return to claim this deduction.
